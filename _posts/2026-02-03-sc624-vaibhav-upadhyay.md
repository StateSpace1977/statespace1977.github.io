## Semester
Spring Semester (Jan - May)

## Prerequisites
Except for the knowledge of linear algebra there is no official prerequisite. But unofficially you would need a strong hold on multivariable calculus and real analysis in order to perform well in the course.

## Grading Criteria (what was marks distribution for exams/assignments/etc..)
One presentation on the topic of your interest, which involves differential geometry (which must be approved by the course instructor).

## Course Content
Review of multivariable calculus
Smooth manifolds
Vector fields, Lie bracket
Frobenius’s theorem, Chow-Rashevsky theorem
Lie groups and Lie algebras
Left- and right-invariant vector fields on Lie groups
Control theory on Lie groups, Feedback linearization
Optimal control on Lie groups
Pontryagin maximum principle on Lie groups

## Feedback on Lectures
Lectures were great.

## Feedback on Assignments/Tutorials/Homework
All you have to do is a project.

## Feedback on Exams
The final exam was a presentation of the work you have studied and your approach to extending it (if applicable). Your evaluation (Oral Q&A) might be on both the lectures and the presentation, so be thorough with both.

## Difficulty (on a scale of 1-5 with 5 being very tough)
4

## Textbooks/References
Here are two nice resources for this course:

1. Lectures on Geometrical Anatomy of Theoretical Physics (YouTube lectures, Prof. Frederic P. Schuller)
2. MAE 276: Geometric Nonlinear Control (YouTube lectures, Prof. Haithem Ezzat)

These resources can partially cover the lectures, since none of them involve the Optimal control part; hence, it's crucial to attend the lectures. The main part of the course is your project, which is in your hands.

## Final Takeaway
Honestly speaking, it's a great course; it has many applications and remains an active area of research. My advice is to take this course only if you want to learn it; otherwise, don't. If you are not at all serious about this course, you will struggle a lot (which I did).
